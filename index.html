Power, status & influence Academic reference
French & Raven (1959) — extended taxonomy
Reward

Reward power

Control over valued outcomes — pay, recognition, privileges. Effective but transient; compliance ends when rewards stop.

Coercive

Coercive power

Ability to punish. Creates compliance but generates resentment and resistance over time.

Legitimate

Legitimate power

Authority derived from a recognized role or title. Depends entirely on others accepting the social structure.

Referent

Referent power

Influence via identification and admiration. The most durable form — operates without direct interaction.

Expert

Expert power

Influence through perceived knowledge or skill. Narrow in scope — expertise in one domain doesn't transfer.

Informational

Informational power

Control over access to knowledge itself, not just expertise. Key in bureaucracies and gatekeeping roles.

Bourdieu's capital framework
Social

Social capital

Network ties and group membership. Who you know and who vouches for you.

Cultural

Cultural capital

Tastes, credentials, manners. Embodied dispositions that signal belonging to dominant groups.

Symbolic

Symbolic capital

Honour, prestige, recognition. Power that disguises itself as merit or distinction.

Status Characteristics Theory (Berger et al., 1977)
Diffuse status characteristics
Broad social categories (gender, race, age) that carry generalized performance expectations. Activate automatically in task groups, creating unequal participation even when irrelevant to the task.
Specific status characteristics
Task-relevant attributes (demonstrated skill, formal credentials). Shape influence but only within domains where they apply.
Status generalization
The key mechanism: high-status individuals are assumed more competent across tasks. The effect persists even when observers are told to ignore the characteristic.
Further status mechanisms
Self-fulfilling

Expectation states

Performance expectations shape actual behavior, creating feedback loops that entrench initial status orderings.

Nonverbal

Status leakage

Posture, eye contact, interruptions, and speaking time all signal and reinforce rank — often below conscious awareness.

Power paradox

The power paradox

Keltner: we gain power through empathy and cooperation, then lose it by becoming less empathic and more self-focused.

Defense

Status threat responses

Threatened status motivates aggressive dominance displays, in-group favoritism, and scapegoating of lower-status targets.

Cialdini's 7 principles of influence
Cialdini

Reciprocity

The obligation to return favors. Uninvited gifts create powerful compliance pressure; the norm is near-universal across cultures.

Cialdini

Commitment & consistency

Once we commit, we align future behavior with that commitment. Small initial agreements escalate. Foot-in-the-door technique.

Cialdini

Social proof

We use others' behavior as information in ambiguous situations. Pluralistic ignorance is a dark side: everyone conforms to a norm no one actually holds.

Cialdini

Authority

Titles, uniforms, and credentials trigger deference. Milgram (1961) showed compliance with lethal commands from perceived authority.

Cialdini

Liking

We comply with people we like. Similarity, physical attractiveness, familiarity, and association all boost liking.

Cialdini

Scarcity

We want what we can't have. Psychological reactance: restrictions on freedom increase perceived desirability.

Cialdini

Unity

Shared identity (family, group, tribe) creates the deepest compliance. "We" — not just liking — drives influence at the identity level.

Other core influence mechanisms
Conformity & normative influence (Asch, 1951)
People deny clear perceptual evidence to agree with a unanimous group. Driven by desire for acceptance, not information.
Minority influence (Moscovici)
Consistent, committed minorities can shift majority opinion over time — a different pathway from majority conformity, operating through deeper processing.
Foundational theorists
FR
French & Raven
Social psychologists, 1950s–60s
Developed the canonical taxonomy of social power bases. Still the most-cited framework in organizational and political psychology.
PB
Pierre Bourdieu
Sociologist, 1930–2002
Field theory, habitus, and capital. Argued power reproduces itself through symbolic misrecognition — dominated groups often unwittingly participate in their own domination.
MF
Michel Foucault
Philosopher, 1926–1984
Power is diffuse, not held — it flows through discourse, institutions, and normalized practices. Discipline and Punish: modern power works through surveillance, not violence.
RC
Robert Cialdini
Social psychologist, 1945–
Identified 7 empirically-grounded principles of influence through field experiments and participant observation. Influence (1984) bridges lab science and applied persuasion.
JB
Joseph Berger et al.
Sociologists, Stanford, 1970s–
Status Characteristics Theory: formalized how socially-valued attributes translate into performance expectations in task groups, explaining persistent inequality in ostensibly meritocratic settings.
DK
Dacher Keltner
Psychologist, UC Berkeley, 1962–
The Power Paradox: power is earned through pro-social behavior but tends to corrupt those who hold it, reducing empathy and impulse control. Evidence from behavioral and neural studies.
SM
Stanley Milgram
Social psychologist, 1933–1984
Obedience to authority experiments (1961–62). ~65% of participants administered apparently lethal shocks when instructed by an authority figure. Foundational for understanding legitimate power and moral disengagement.
SF
Susan Fiske
Social psychologist, Princeton, 1952–
Stereotype Content Model: groups are stereotyped on warmth × competence axes, predicting envy, admiration, contempt, or pity. Power and status are indexed along the competence dimension.
